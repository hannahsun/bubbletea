bubblepedia ——

Since bubble tea being originated in Taiwan in the 1980’s, the bubble tea fad continues to spread, and now it’s gaining popularity beyond the Asian community into Europe and North America.





But what exactly is
bubble tea?

In its most basic form, the drink consists of black tea, milk, ice, and chewy tapioca pearls, all shaken together like a martini and served with that famously fat straw to accommodate the marbles of tapioca that cluster at the bottom of the cup. Since its beginnings, the basic tapioca iced tea recipe has evolved into an entire genre of drinks.

What’s in the name?

It’s a common and understandable misconception that bubble tea gets its name from the bubble-like tapioca pearls that fill the bottom of the cup. Rather, the truth behind the origin of the bubble tea name can be seen at the top of the beverage. When you order a bubble tea, the mixture is shaken vigorously, by hand or by machine, before being served. This shaking process creates foamy bubbles at the top of the drink, and it’s this uppermost layer that gives bubble tea its name.

Bubble tea is also commonly called boba tea, pearl tea, or tapioca tea. The monikers differ by location, but also personal preference. In the U.S., the East Coast favors bubble tea, while the West prefers boba.

Did you know that bubble tea is quickly becoming a new craze at New York City?

A growing number of bubble tea shops are opening throughout the metropolitan region.
In an attempt to introduce you to this drink, we've outlined some of the most popular bubble teas and the various choices you have when ordering or making bubble tea.

info ——

A milky tea with tapioca pearls is commonly known as one of the classic bubble tea. Traditionally 1/12th-inch in diameter and usually black in color, tapioca pearl is made from cassava starch, sweet potato and brown sugar. Sometimes white tapioca pearls are used—these are made from cassava starch, caramel, and chamomile root and have different flavor.

Any type of milk tea with any type of pudding is bliss. Milk tea and pudding are a match made in Heaven. But if you’re getting a fruit slush, topping it with pudding might not be a good idea...

Red bean, also known as Azuki in Japanese, is sweet, creamy and earthy. It has a slight granulated texture and goes great with dairy.

Another alternative to traditional bubble tea is to substitute tapioca pearls with coconut jelly, a lighter option. Coconut jelly is served in small Lego-like pieces and have a sweet, crunchy consistency

Grass Jelly is a jelly-like dessert eaten in East Asia. It’s created by using the Platostoma palustre plant and has a milky, slightly bitter taste.

The regular milk tea in the market is made with non-dairy creamer to create a rich and creamy texture and flavor.

Tea latte is made with real dairy or non-dairy milk. A hot tea latte, like cafe lattes, is typically made with steamed milk. It can also be served cold, which sometimes is called ‘Fresh Milk Tea’.

Black tea is the most classic, and by far, the most popular tea base for bubble tea. If it's your first time having bubble tea or if you're not really in the adventurous mood, this is a great starter drink.

Jasmine green tea and matcha powder are the two major green tea options for bubble tea. This is another flavor for tea lovers who prefer green tea over black tea.

Oolong is also one of the most prevalent base teas for bubble tea. The amount of caffeine it contains is greater than green tea but less than black tea.

01
Boba Guys
bobaguys.com

This San Franciscan company has gone cross-country with stores on the Lower East Side, Greenwich Village and Canal Street Market. Pushing aside powders, their drinks go all natural with tea leaves, organic milk and house-made syrups. Menu lists range from classic milk tea and Jasmine milk tea to their Dirty Horchata, topped with espresso, and the Strawberry Matcha Latte.

02
Gong Cha
gongchausa.com

Their house brand specialty is what’s called a “milk foam,” described as a “slightly salted cream that sits on top of freshly brewed tea.” As for the Taiwan-sourced teas, popular flavors are the Pearl Milk Tea (their version of black milk tea), Milk Foam Green Tea, Taro Milk Tea and Lychee Oolong.

03
Tiger Sugar
en.tigersugar.com

This cult-favorite Taiwanese chain attracted lines up to three hours long at its first North American foray in Flushing, Queens. Devoted Tiger Sugar fans clamor for the brown sugar boba milk with cream mousse, whose tiger-like stripes of dark brown sugar syrup and milky tea dripping down the sides of the cup make it a photogenic bestseller.

04
YiFang Taiwan Fruit Tea
yifangteausa.com

Yi Fang’s niche is fresh fruit tea that originated from the founder’s great grandma’s recipe on a pineapple farm in Taiwan. The signature Yi Fang fruit tea contains fresh orange, apple, pineapple, and passionfruit. The most popular topping is limited-batch, brown sugar pearls; they’re served after 1 p.m. until the store runs out, and unlike other drinks here, the sweetness and ice levels are fixed. The other, customizable drinks here tend to go hard on the sugar, so scale down on sweetness.

05
CoCo Fresh Tea & Juice
cocobubbletea.com

With 22 stores throughout NYC, this global brand from Taiwan switches up the teas stateside by introducing drinks that suit American palettes. As for customers on this side of the globe, they apparently savor their nutty taro milk tea and floral Jasmine milk tea. And tapioca bubbles are kept within their optimal taste period to make certain they’re nice and chewy.

06
Kung Fu Tea
kungfutea.com

Founded by three friends in Flushing in 2009, this chain has developed into the Starbucks of bubble tea, with more than 100 locations nationwide. Kung Fu Tea offers simple, standard milk teas made with tea, milk powder, and bubbles cooked every two hours. The menu includes less ubiquitous offerings like wintermelon milk green tea, purple taro, sweet red-bean slush, and yogurt-based drinks made with the tart Japanese Yakult drink.

07
KOI Thé
koithe.com/en

Koi Thé with locations across Asia and the U.S., now has a shop on a busy corner of Bleecker Street and MacDougal, in Greenwich Village. Just look for the giant cup of a bubble tea in front with the shop’s signature golden bubbles. Koi Thé is also known for its macchiato teas in flavors like black, green, mango and peach. Other offerings include tea latte, milk tea and “chewy” tea. Other flavored teas include Jasmine Green Tea, Assam Black Tea and Plum Green Tea.

08
Tea and Milk
teaandmilk.com/

This cozy Astoria storefront also skips artificial ingredients and instead uses teas from all over the world, along with spices and fruits. Their Signature Taro Root Milk Tea mingles pureed taro with black tea nicely, while their Roasted Oolong Tea with Signature Cream unleashes creamy goodness upon a good shaking.
